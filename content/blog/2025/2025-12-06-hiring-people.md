Hiring the right people is one of the most critical decisions any organization makes. The process of finding, evaluating, and selecting candidates can shape the future of a team, project, or entire company.

<!-- more -->

# Who should you hire ?

Hire smart people. We don't hire people to tell them what to do, but for them to tell us what needs to be done. It's important to identify the smartest people during the hiring process, questions can range from logical problems to technical issues and how to solve them.

Hire passionate people. It's important to find people who are in love with the problems and solutions that we are dealing with. Passionate people will drive themselves, the only requirement is for the manager or the company to provide the vision and purpose.

Hire different people. Diversity leads to innovation and critical thinking. Hiring diverse profiles builds a team that challenges solutions and approaches instead of mostly agreeing with them. This devil's advocate approach ensures that the solutions and proposals will be the best possible, putting everything to the test.

Hire collaborative people. When working in development, be sure not to hire lone wolves. Typically, their work follows their own standards, their own rules, their own approaches. When developing as a team, the steps need to be calculated in a way that communicates what is being done, the work itself communicates intentions to others. Development should be collaborative, with feedback, questions, and the input of others.

# Soft / Hard skills

On day to day technical skills shine, allow developers to solve immediate problems, write code, fix bugs, and deliver features. However, in the long run soft-skills make a huge difference, it’s what enables the team to function effectively, with communication, organization, collaboration.. It helps the team to self-organize and be self-managed.

We don’t work in isolation, we function as a team, almost like an organism within a company, where soft skills define how we organize, communicate, and operate as a unified entity. Soft skills are, in my opinion, more important than technical knowledge, since technical skills can be taught, while behavioral skills, shaped by life experience, family, culture, friendships, successes, and failures, are much harder to develop, especially in organizations filled with engineers. The ability to understand oneself, communicate clearly, navigate conflicts, and appreciate the diversity of perspectives is not only crucial in a professional environment, but also in life in general, people who possess strong soft skills and self-knowledge are likely to thrive in any environment, regardless of their technical expertise.
